What STEPS is—and is not

STEPS is a decision aid. It translates programme design choices (tier, mentorship, delivery, qualification, cost, scale) into: endorsement, perceived programme value, economic costs, and capacity needs (mentors). It can also show illustrative epidemiological benefits when assumptions are provided.

Epidemiological outputs are scenario modelling, not a forecast. They depend on assumptions that should be replaced with local evidence for high-stakes decisions.

Part A — For policymakers and non-technical FETP audiences

If you only read one section, read this.

What decision questions does STEPS answer?

Which design is most acceptable?

Endorsement changes across tiers, mentorship intensity, delivery modes, and qualifications.

What is “value” from the user’s perspective?

Perceived programme value is derived from stated preference trade-offs, expressed as willingness-to-pay (WTP).

Is it feasible to scale—right now?

Mentor requirements and shortfalls are computed automatically from cohort size and mentorship intensity.

What happens when you click Apply configuration

STEPS checks feasibility of horizon and cohort count for the selected tier, then recalculates all outputs.
Tier duration constraints
Frontline duration = 3 months ⇒ max cohorts = floor(horizon_months / 3)
Intermediate duration = 12 months ⇒ max cohorts = floor(horizon_months / 12)
Advanced duration = 24 months ⇒ horizon must be ≥ 24 months; max cohorts = floor(horizon_months / 24)
Response time is fixed to 7 days (rapid response benchmark) for consistent comparisons.
2) Settings tab — “Which assumptions drive the results the most?”

Settings is where programme teams update assumptions when new evidence arrives (e.g., mentor staffing plans, revised costing, revised outbreak value assumptions).

Setting areaWhat it affectsTypical reason to change it

Cost templateTier-specific opportunity cost add-on, plus cost shares for reporting.A new costing study changes overhead/opportunity structure.
Epidemiological settingsOutbreak value; outbreaks per graduate; completion rates; discount rate.Alignment with national costing guidance or new effectiveness evidence.
If you want STEPS to focus only on acceptability + perceived programme value, you can treat epidemiology as “off” by setting outbreak value to 0.
3) Results tab — “Which options dominate on value-for-money?”

Results summarises the currently applied configuration. It highlights endorsement, perceived value, economic costs, and a simple “configuration status” label.

The status label uses two thresholds: endorsement ≥ 50% and the benefit–cost ratio (BCR). A “Challenging configuration” label usually means BCR is below 1.0 (under the current benefit assumptions), even if endorsement is moderate.
4) National simulation tab — “What does this imply at national scale?”

This tab translates your scenario into indicative national requirements, focusing on mentor requirements and capacity.

Required mentors nationally = required_mentors_per_cohort × number_of_cohorts

5) Sensitivity tab — “When do conclusions change, and when don’t they?”

Sensitivity explores “what-if” changes around perceived programme value and endorsement-adjusted value. Epidemiological columns are intentionally removed in this view to keep the sensitivity discussion focussed on value perceptions.

6) Saved scenarios tab — “How do we compare options side-by-side?”

Save scenarios when you want a comparison set for meetings. Each saved scenario stores the full configuration and computed outputs. STEPS also stores Enablers and Risks as standard decision-ready content.

Enablers (auto-filled examples):
• Leverage NCDC stewardship
• Standardised curriculum and mentorship
• Phased expansion using hubs
• Incentives aligned with career pathways
Risks (auto-filled examples):
• Mentor availability constraints
• Uneven site readiness
• Delays in incentive payments
• Retention challenges
• Need for quality assurance as scale grows
7) Copilot/ChatGPT tab — “How do we turn results into a policy brief?”

This tab prints a clean snapshot of results first, then generates structured prompts for Copilot/ChatGPT. The prompts are written to keep tables aligned and to include Enablers/Risks automatically.

Prompts are meaningful only after at least one configuration has been applied (and ideally saved). If no scenario exists, STEPS should show a warning before copying.

Part B — For technical readers

This section maps directly to the computations implemented in script.js.

How is endorsement calculated?

Endorsement is computed as a logit probability of choosing the programme over an opt-out.

U(programme) = ASC_programme + β_tier + β_qualification + β_mentorship + β_delivery + β_response + β_cost × (cost/1000)
U(opt-out) = ASC_optout
P(endorse) = exp(U(programme)) / (exp(U(programme)) + exp(U(opt-out)))

How is willingness-to-pay (WTP) derived?

WTP (₹/month) = (U_non-cost / |β_cost|) × 1000

How are programme costs calculated?

Programme cost per cohort = cost_per_trainee_per_month × duration_months × trainees_per_cohort
Opportunity cost add-on = Programme cost × opp_rate(tier)
Total economic cost per cohort = Programme cost + opportunity cost + mentor support cost

How are illustrative epidemiological benefits computed?

Effective graduates = trainees_per_cohort × completion_rate × endorsement_rate
Outbreaks per year per cohort = effective_graduates × outbreaks_per_grad_per_year × response_multiplier
PV factor (years n, rate r) = (1 − (1+r)^(-n)) / r
PV outbreak benefit per cohort = outbreaks_per_year × value_per_outbreak × PV factor
Default parameters (completion rate, outbreaks per graduate, outbreak value, discount rate) are placeholders. Replace with agreed evidence before using BCR/NPV in external reporting.

Scale-up recommendations to meet the IHR/JEE workforce target

This section turns the benchmark into a concrete plan (cohorts, horizon, mentors).

What is the benchmark—and how large is the gap?

A commonly cited workforce benchmark for IHR/JEE capacity is 1 trained field epidemiologist per 200,000 population (see Jones et al., 2017). Using that ratio:

Population assumptionBenchmark target (1 per 200,000)Baseline trained*Gap
1.3 billion (older planning assumption)6,5003,3003,200
1.4 billion (approx. current scale)7,0003,3003,700
*The “~3,300 trained” figure was provided in the briefing text. A publicly citable source could not be confirmed here; replace with an audited programme total for external-facing materials.

What does the ‘One‑India’ roadmap imply for how to scale?

NCDC describes the ‘One India’ FETP roadmap as a phased expansion under NCDC stewardship aiming to extend training coverage to all districts by 2030. In STEPS terms, this implies: (i) multi-hub delivery, (ii) standardised mentorship, and (iii) a deliberate mentor pipeline.

What are practical cohort, horizon, and mentor targets?

A practical plan is to treat Intermediate and Advanced as the benchmark workforce pipeline, while using Frontline to support rapid district coverage. The numbers below are a starting point to stress-test in STEPS; refine them until mentor shortfalls are manageable.

Plan A: close ~3,200 (from a 6,500 target)

Intermediate5 cohorts/year × 5 years = 2540~900Use Medium mentorship where feasible; build mentor pipeline early.
Advanced1 cohort/year × 5 years = 580~360High mentorship increases mentor requirements sharply; prioritise quality assurance and retention.

Plan B: close ~3,700 (from ~7,000 target)

TierCohorts (2026–2030)Trainees / cohortApprox. graduates*Mentor capacity implication

How to compute “additional mentors needed” in STEPS terms
mentors_per_cohort = ceil(trainees_per_cohort ÷ mentor_capacity)
mentors_needed_nationally = mentors_per_cohort × number_of_cohorts
mentor_shortfall = max(0, mentors_needed_nationally − available_mentors_nationally)

Example: Intermediate + Medium mentorship (capacity 3.5) + 45 trainees/cohort ⇒ mentors_per_cohort = ceil(45/3.5)=13. If 6 cohorts run nationally, mentors_needed_nationally=78. Compare this to the “available mentors nationally” setting.

Policy implication: mentor constraints are the tightest bottleneck. A scalable plan usually combines (i) staged growth in cohorts, (ii) cohort sizing that respects mentor capacity, and (iii) explicit investment in mentor development and retention.

*Graduates shown above are indicative, assuming 90% completion and endorsement already accounted for in programme uptake planning. Use STEPS to test alternative completion, endorsement, and mentorship intensity assumptions.

How should programme teams update assumptions when new evidence arrives?

Treat STEPS as a living decision aid. When new evidence arrives (e.g., a costing study, mentor workload study, or programme effectiveness evaluation), update the relevant Settings, then re-run scenarios. For transparency, record: (i) old value, (ii) new value, (iii) evidence source, and (iv) meeting date.
